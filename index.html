Tesla Cybertruck

When bold engineering bets outrun the market

We picked the Cybertruck not because it was a success story, but because it wasn't. A product that was genuinely innovative and genuinely misaligned with its market at the same time - that's where the interesting PM questions live.

The core finding

Tesla didn't fail at engineering. They failed at translation. The stainless exoskeleton eliminated a $400M paint shop investment - a factory win. But it also created $20,000+ repair costs and shut out the third-party service ecosystem - a customer loss. Manufacturing innovation and product innovation are not the same thing.

Vision
Collapse utility, performance and lifestyle into one truck - instead of competing incrementally with F-150
Tradeoff
Stainless exoskeleton - unbreakable identity, but unaffordable repairs. A decision that served the launch story and hurt the lifecycle
Risk
48% sales decline in 2025. Early adopters converted, mainstream didn't. Recall fatigue and insurance costs did the rest
Recommendation
Dual roadmap: keep the radical flagship as a halo product, launch a pragmatic variant under $50K for fleet and contractor segments

Nvidia Rubin Platform

Control over scale: why the best allocation decision isn't the biggest revenue decision

Most strategy reports on Nvidia ask "how do they sell more chips." We asked a different question: in a market where demand already exceeds supply at any price, what should allocation actually optimize for? The answer we landed on was uncomfortable - and we think it's right.

The core argument

Rubin isn't a GPU generation. It's a decade-long infrastructure commitment disguised as a product launch. Every allocation decision Nvidia makes is an alliance decision - and treating it like a sales decision is the strategic error. We recommended deprioritizing hyperscalers in Year 1 entirely, even though they're Nvidia's three largest customers. Because they're also the three most dangerous long-term competitors.

Positioning
Reframe Rubin from "GPU generation" to "sovereign compute infrastructure" - the unit of compute has shifted from chip to data center
Allocation
Sovereign nations first, frontier AI labs second, hyperscalers intentionally excluded in Year 1 - because they can't credibly threaten to leave
Pricing
No public list price. Opaque, tiered, rack-only. Price as a diplomatic instrument, not a market-clearing mechanism
GTM
Rubin should be revealed, not launched. Private pre-briefings 6 months out, GTC-only reveal - not CES. Zero competitor comparisons in official comms
